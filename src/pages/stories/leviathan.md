> Water reveals patterns in stone. What do those patterns show us?

A muted shadow hung on the breeze like a billowing cloak and dark storm clouds loomed on the horizon. The splash of brine and sea ricocheted off of the stone coastline, leaving the sermon’s audience in a damp sheen. It was cold, far too cold. The waters here should have long frozen over, but they never did. The Ardentia still held no answers as to why, but the priest, deep down in his gut, knew. All who were present at the sermon knew. This was sacred ground.

The storm approached at the priest’s back and lightning cracked across the sky.

“The Leviathan wakes my friends!”, the priest bellowed, “With each passing storm he shows himself to us!”

The priest gestured out to an enormous boulder that pierced through the crashing waves. Centuries washed it clean, stripping layers of stone from the surface and rounding the rock’s edges. Smaller rocks had long been torn from the boulder’s hard carapace. Embedded within was a vast skeleton of pure white bone. The spine curved along the rock’s edge closest to the coast in a great arch. It’s tail wove in and out of the rock and tips of what could be fangs and claws pierced the surface and were as long daggers. Despite the weathering that the rock endured, the bone remained polished, pure, and pristine. When lightning struck, the glow of the storms almost seemed to linger on the old bones.

Every week the priest asked the same question at his sermon. He bellowed over the harsh winds that buffeted against the thick cloaks of the townspeople.

“Which among you? Which among you have iron for bone?”

A small cloaked figure stepped forward and lowered his hood to face the priest. He couldn’t have been more than ten years of age, and hardly a man. Yet, his eyes were of steel and he met the priest’s gaze with honor.

“And who might you be young one?”, the priest asked.

“Evan”, the small boy said. He took another step forward.

The priest nodded gravely and took the boy’s hand. He ironed himself for what must be done. After so many storms he had never grown accustomed to the ritual, but it had to be done. It never grew easier in the moment, but it had grown easier to drown himself in drink when the storm had finally passed over. 

It was that look of bravery they all shared when they stepped forward, man and child alike. For he saw what the others did not, the snapping of that bravery when he strung them up to the post at sea. Luckily for the priest, and his followers, the stormwinds muted their screams as they died and the Leviathan claimed them.
